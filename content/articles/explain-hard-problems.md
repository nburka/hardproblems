We were asked to present about Hard Problems at the wonderful [Leading Design](https://leadingdesign.com) conference in London in November 2025. Thank you to the team at [Clearleft](https://clearleft.com) for the invitation.

## The talk {#the-talk}

My career spans back to the late 1990s — including the sale of my startup, Milk, to Google and a subsequent tenure at Google Ventures where I co-authored the book _Sprint_. I drew on that experience to share what I've learned along the way.

While I don't think corporate tech jobs entirely "poison" individual talent, I argued that advertising-reliant business models inevitably dictate and compromise the desire to optimize for what's best for the world at most organizations.

## Is corporate work the only option? {#is-corporate-work-the-only-option}

The crux of my talk focused on a systemic misalignment between professional aspirations and market realities. I recalled a recent visit to the [Royal College of Art](https://www.rca.ac.uk/), where service design students presented sophisticated projects for problems like the diabetes care pathway and service design for support systems for victims of sexual assault. Almost none of them will be working on those problems — economics routinely funnel top-tier young designers into corporate roles.

Mahima echoed this frustration, noting that while human-centered design programs in Bangalore train students to tackle community health crises and childhood malnutrition, the market offers virtually no employment for such roles upon graduation. This structural disconnect, I argued, underpins a widespread "malaise" and historic burnout within the design community, as highly compensated tech workers increasingly realize their labor yields little societal value compared to lower-paid professions like nursing or teaching.

Invoking the Japanese philosophical framework of [Ikigai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ikigai), I asserted that while contemporary designers successfully align what they love, what they excel at, and what secures them high salaries, they fundamentally neglect what the world actually needs. I emphasized my personal ethos by referencing a tattoo on my wrist: "Don't make anything unless it's necessary, but if it's necessary, don't hesitate to make it beautiful."

## Examples from the field {#examples-from-the-field}

To demonstrate the viability of humanitarian tech, I shared my last eight years leading [Simple](https://simple.org), an open-source longitudinal medical record engineered alongside former CDC Director [Dr. Tom Frieden](https://drtomfrieden.net/). Deployed in countries like Bangladesh, where primary care clinicians will manage up to 120 patients a day at a pace of one patient every two minutes, the interface was rigorously designed to record data in under 13 seconds, successfully returning the doctor's focus back to the patient and driving critical feedback loops into the health system that actually create systemic improvement in patient outcomes.

Mahima introduced a second case study: a mobile-based screening application fighting oral cancer in rural India. The disease claims five Indian lives every day due to the widespread cultural practice of chewing betel nut, a potent carcinogen. To bridge the gap where rural patients have zero access to oncology specialists, Mahima's team designed an intuitive app that enables frontline healthcare workers — many unfamiliar with smartphones — to capture oral cavity photos for automated AI risk assessment and immediate clinical referral.

Mahima also outlined an ongoing climate preparedness initiative with a regional disaster management unit to combat lethal heatwaves across India. While affluent citizens rely on air-conditioned offices, outdoor laborers like fishermen and farmers face skyrocketing rates of heatstroke, rage, and suicide. The project relies on behavior and information design to ensure critical weather data smoothly travels from meteorology stations directly into vulnerable communities, allowing them to dynamically alter their daily routines.

## Hard problems are hard {#hard-problems-are-hard}

I explicitly cautioned the audience against technological hubris, citing historical missteps like the "One Laptop per Child" program as examples of arrogant developers expecting hardware to solve deeply rooted poverty. Complex humanitarian problems cannot be fixed simply by throwing isolated code at them; they require an understanding of macroeconomics, local politics, and long-term, multi-decade iterations with local stakeholders.

I didn't minimize the real financial trade-offs required to engage in this work — civic tech will never compete with the hundreds of thousands of dollars Silicon Valley pays designers to optimize search advertisements. Furthermore, because the practitioners operating in global public health humbly keep their heads down rather than chasing viral engagement on LinkedIn or TikTok, these career paths remain largely invisible to the public.

## It's possible to change the culture of design {#its-possible-to-change-the-culture-of-design}

In a final call to action, I urged the design community to establish a unified professional ethos, explicitly comparing the moment to the American legal revolution of the late 1960s, when a generation of Ivy League lawyers left corporate firms to join "Nader's Raiders" and successfully pass landmark safety and environmental laws.

I closed by introducing Hard Problems, which will deploy workshops and specialized assessment squads to help purpose-driven technologists build tools that actively change the world.
